Vanilla Beans, Caviar, or Paste: Which Should You Use?

6 min read — Vanilla

Walk down the baking aisle and "vanilla" can mean at least four different things before you even get to extract. Whole beans, scraped caviar, spoonable paste, and pure extract all come from the same cured pod — a Madagascar bourbon vanilla bean — but they behave very differently once they hit a mixing bowl. Here's how to tell them apart, and when each one earns its place in a recipe.

Whole vanilla bean pods

A whole pod is the entire cured fruit of the vanilla orchid — a climbing vine that has to be hand-pollinated the day its flower opens, then cured and dried for months before it develops that familiar dark, oily, fragrant character. Nothing has been added or taken away.

Split a pod lengthwise with the tip of a knife and you'll find thousands of tiny black seeds packed inside. Scrape them out with the back of the blade and both the seeds and the empty pod are useful: the seeds carry the concentrated aroma, while the spent pod still holds plenty of flavour and is worth steeping in milk, cream, or sugar rather than throwing away.

Best for: custards, crème brûlée, ice cream bases, and anything where the vanilla is meant to be the star and those signature black flecks are part of the presentation.

Vanilla caviar

Vanilla caviar is simply the scraped seeds on their own — all of the aroma and visual appeal of a whole pod, with none of the woody pod fibre left behind. It's what you get if someone has already done the splitting and scraping for you, in concentrated form.

Because there's no pod bulking it out, a little goes further than you'd expect, and it blends into a batter or cream far more smoothly than diced pod fragments would. It's a genuine time-saver for anyone who wants the pure ingredient without the prep work.

Best for: whipped cream, glazes, buttercream, and any recipe where you want the caviar to disappear into a smooth mixture rather than infuse over time.

Vanilla bean paste with honey

Paste takes things a step further for convenience: real scraped vanilla seeds are blended with vanilla extract and a thickener (in our case, honey) into a spoonable consistency. You get the flecks and depth of a real pod, measured out like a liquid extract — no splitting, no scraping, no simmering required.

Because it's pre-mixed with a liquid base, paste behaves more like extract in a recipe: it disperses quickly rather than needing time to infuse, which makes it the most forgiving option for a home baker working on a deadline.

Best for: everyday baking — cookies, cakes, frostings — anywhere a recipe calls for "vanilla extract" but you'd like the visual and flavour upgrade of real bean flecks without extra steps.

So which one should you buy?

If you only keep one vanilla product in the pantry, paste is the most versatile — it substitutes 1:1 for extract in almost any recipe while still delivering real bean flavour. Keep whole pods on hand for the recipes where you want that dramatic split-pod presentation, and caviar for when you want pure, concentrated seeds without any pod texture at all.

All three start from the same thing: a well-cured Madagascar bourbon vanilla bean. The form you choose changes how it behaves in the bowl, not the quality of what's inside it.
