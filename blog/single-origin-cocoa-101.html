Single-Origin Cocoa 101: From Bean to Powder

6 min read — Cocoa

"Single-origin" gets used loosely in food marketing, but on a cocoa label it has a specific, meaningful definition — and it's a big part of why some cocoa powders taste like flat, bitter dust while others taste like actual chocolate before any sugar has been added.

What "single-origin" actually means

Most commercial cocoa is a blend: beans from several countries, sometimes several continents, mixed together to produce a consistent, neutral flavour at scale. That consistency comes at a cost — blending averages out the distinctive character any one growing region would otherwise contribute.

Single-origin cocoa comes from one country, often one growing region within it, and isn't blended with beans from anywhere else. That means the flavour in the bag genuinely reflects the soil, climate, and cacao variety of that specific place — the same logic that applies to single-origin coffee or single-vineyard wine.

From pod to powder

Cacao starts as a large, football-shaped pod growing directly on the trunk of a cacao tree. Inside, the beans are surrounded by a white, faintly sweet pulp. After harvest, the beans are fermented in that pulp for several days — this step matters enormously, since fermentation is where a lot of chocolate's characteristic flavour actually develops.

After fermenting, the beans are dried, roasted to develop deeper flavour, then cracked and ground. Roasted, ground cacao produces a thick paste called cocoa liquor, from which cocoa butter (the fat) can be pressed out, leaving a dry cake behind. That cake is what gets milled into cocoa powder.

Why stone-milling matters

Most industrial cocoa powder is milled with high-speed steel machinery that generates heat, which can flatten the more delicate aromatic compounds in the bean. Stone-milling grinds more slowly and at lower temperatures, preserving more of the fruitier, more complex notes that a fast industrial process tends to cook away.

The result is a powder that still carries some of the fruit and roast character of the original bean, rather than tasting uniformly bitter and one-dimensional.

Why unsweetened doesn't mean bland

Unsweetened cocoa has a reputation for being harsh, which is really a reflection of low-quality, over-processed cocoa rather than a property of unsweetened cocoa itself. A well-made, single-origin, stone-milled cocoa powder has enough natural depth — chocolate notes, subtle fruit undertones — that it doesn't need sugar to taste like something. Sugar is there to balance bitterness, not to manufacture flavour that isn't there to begin with.

How to use it

Bloom cocoa powder in a small amount of hot liquid before adding it to a recipe — whisking it into warm milk, melted butter, or hot water for even a minute unlocks far more aroma than adding dry powder straight into a batter. It's a small step that makes a noticeable difference in anything from a cake to a simple cocoa milkshake.
